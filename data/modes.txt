MUSICAL MODES AND THEIR MOODS

A mode is a type of scale with a distinct pattern of intervals and a characteristic mood. The seven modes are derived from the major scale by starting on different notes. Each mode has its own emotional flavor.

Ionian is the standard major scale. It sounds bright, happy, and stable.

Dorian is a minor mode with a raised sixth note. It sounds minor but slightly brighter and more hopeful than natural minor, often described as jazzy, soulful, or cool. Common in folk, rock, and jazz.

Phrygian is a minor mode with a lowered second note. It sounds dark, exotic, tense, and is often associated with Spanish flamenco and metal music.

Lydian is a major mode with a raised fourth note. It sounds dreamy, floating, magical, or cinematic. Often used in film scores.

Mixolydian is a major mode with a lowered seventh note. It sounds bluesy, rootsy, and slightly unresolved. Common in rock, blues, and folk.

Aeolian is the natural minor scale. It sounds sad, melancholic, and serious.

Locrian is a rare, unstable mode with a diminished tonic chord. It sounds tense, dissonant, and unresolved, and is seldom used as a tonal center.

Choosing a mode instead of a plain major or minor scale is a way to give a progression a specific, distinctive emotional color.
